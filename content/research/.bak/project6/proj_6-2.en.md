## Quantum Chemistry and Quantum Computing

### Toward Breakthroughs in Quantum Chemical Calculations

The microscopic world in which molecules operate is governed by the laws of quantum mechanics.
Essential chemical reactions in living systems proceed through complex interactions among large biomolecules—such as proteins, nucleic acids, lipids, and glycans—and numerous water molecules, ions, and small molecules.
However, as the number of molecules and atoms increases, the computational cost required for quantum chemical calculations grows exponentially.
As a result, it is practically impossible with current computers to treat large and complex systems consisting of biomolecules and their surrounding environments entirely on the basis of rigorous quantum mechanics.

To overcome this limitation, MD methods—where interatomic and intermolecular interactions are approximated as force fields and molecular motions are described within the framework of classical mechanics—and QM/MM approach—where only the key reactive regions are treated quantum mechanically—have been widely used.
Nevertheless, these approaches are inherently approximate. For a fundamental understanding of chemical reactions occurring in living systems, it is ideal to describe the entire large and complex molecular system based directly on quantum mechanics.

Quantum computers operate on principles fundamentally different from those of classical computers and are expected to enable (1) highly accurate and (2) large-scale quantum chemical calculations.
For this reason, they are attracting great interest as a technology that could bring about breakthroughs in the field of biochemistry.

### 1. Improving the Accuracy of Quantum Chemical Calculations

Current quantum chemical calculations rely on numerous approximations.
As a result, their accuracy is subject to intrinsic limitations, and it is particularly challenging to accurately describe phenomena that exhibit strong quantum-mechanical character.
Quantum computers, by their very nature, can directly execute calculations based on quantum mechanics itself, and are therefore expected to enable highly accurate quantum chemical calculations that have been difficult to achieve with conventional classical computers.
Such improvements in accuracy are essential for correctly understanding phenomena that are critically important for biological function yet have been difficult to treat accurately due to their pronounced quantum nature—such as light absorption and excited states, bond rearrangements, electron transfer, and proton transfer.

### 2. Application to Large-Scale Systems

In biomolecular systems, long-range interactions as well as electron and proton transfer processes spanning both intra- and intermolecular scales play essential roles in functional expression.
In conventional QM/MM approaches, the region treated quantum mechanically is limited to a local area, making it difficult to analyze such long-range effects.

If quantum computers make it possible to treat larger molecular systems—including entire proteins and their surrounding environments—quantum chemically “as a whole,” interactions and changes in charge distributions that previously had to be approximated can be described in a unified manner.
This would enable a more fundamental understanding of the detailed mechanisms of reactions in enzymes, ion pumps and channels, and electron transfer systems.

Furthermore, large-scale quantum chemical calculations of this kind are expected to play an important role in the design of materials and pharmaceuticals.
By enabling high-accuracy predictions of the electronic states and reactivity of entire molecules, rational molecular design becomes possible, providing new guiding principles for materials science and drug discovery.

### Summary

In this way, while quantum computers hold great potential to bring breakthroughs to the field of biochemistry, large-scale quantum computers are still under development.
By continuously monitoring advances in quantum computing and actively integrating the knowledge accumulated through research using classical computers, we aim to contribute to the further advancement of biomolecular science.
