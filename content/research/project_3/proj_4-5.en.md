## Glycan structure

Glycans are biomolecules made up of monosaccharides linked in chain-like structures, and in living systems, they are commonly attached to proteins or lipids (Fig. 1). About 50% of eukaryotic proteins are glycosylated, and for membrane proteins, glycans are known to be attached to most of them. Glycans bound to membrane proteins play vital roles in biological processes such as cell–cell communication and disease mechanisms, including viral infections. Therefore, accurately identifying glycan structures—especially those related to biological function and disease—can enhance our understanding of life processes and support medical and drug discovery research.

However, precise structural analysis of glycans is difficult because they have branched structures and many isomeric forms. Ion mobility–mass spectrometry (IM-MS) is a technique that identifies biomolecules based on their shape, measured by collision cross sections (CCSs). With recent improvements in sensitive instruments, IM-MS has become a valuable method for separating glycan isomers that only subtly differ in their three-dimensional shapes. At the same time, the presence of multiple stable conformations for a single glycan makes interpreting CCS values more complex.

In this study, we examined ten pyridylaminated N-linked branched glycans (Fig. 2(a), left) and developed a method to predict CCS values from conformational ensembles generated by molecular dynamics (MD) simulations. While earlier research has attempted to infer molecular structures from CCS measurements, no work had systematically predicted CCS values based on conformational ensembles. Using replica-exchange MD, an efficient conformational sampling technique, we consistently generated structural ensembles and enabled quantitative CCS predictions. Representative structures and their populations were identified through cluster analysis [7], and CCS values for each structure were calculated using MOBCAL [8]. The CCS values obtained demonstrated a strong correlation with experimental data, with a correlation coefficient of 0.9 (Fig. 2(a), right).

Structural analysis showed that N-linked glycans in the gas phase tend to adopt characteristic folded shapes depending on their protonation states and branch lengths (Fig. 2(b)). The so-called “back-fold” structure, in which the α1–6 arm bends back, has relatively large CCS values, while a compact “globular” shape—where all branches cluster together—has notably smaller CCS values (Fig. 2(c)). For most glycans, one stable conformation usually dominates the ensemble, indicating that measured CCS values closely reflect the underlying folding features.

These results not only facilitate the interpretation of IM-MS data but also show that CCS values for new glycans can be predicted using a combination of MD simulations and MOBCAL calculations. Further integration with ab initio MD [9] is expected to enable more accurate predictions that explicitly account for changes in protonation states. Ultimately, the synergy between MD simulations and IM-MS measurements is expected to greatly advance the identification and functional characterization of biologically relevant glycans.
