## Spike Protein

### Conformational Dynamics of the SARS-CoV-2 Spike Protein

We have findings from two molecular dynamics (MD) simulation studies on the SARS-CoV-2 spike (S) protein, a primary target for vaccines and antiviral drugs. The research provides an atomistic-level understanding of the S-protein's structural flexibility, the mechanisms governing its transition between inactive ("Down") and active ("Up") states, and the implications for therapeutic intervention.

We have the following key takeaways:

1. Inherent Flexibility and Multiple Conformations: The S-protein is intrinsically dynamic, spontaneously transitioning between multiple conformations without requiring a ligand. Enhanced sampling simulations identified not only the known Down and one-Up states but also previously hidden functional structures, including intermediate states, a "one-Open" (1UO) state with maximum receptor binding domain (RBD) exposure, and "two-Up-like" (2UL) structures.
2. The "Glycan-Locking Mechanism": N-glycans play a crucial, dual role in regulating RBD movement. Specific glycans (notably at positions N165, N234, and N343) act as molecular switches, stabilizing certain conformations while driving transitions to others. The N343 glycan helps initiate the Down-to-Up transition, N165 acts as a barrier stabilizing the Down state, and N234 stabilizes the final Up conformation.
3. A Multi-Step Transition Pathway: The transition from the inactive Down state to the active one-Up state is not a simple binary switch. It proceeds through at least two stable intermediate conformations. This pathway is initiated by the flexibility of the Down state and driven by transient salt-bridge formations between RBDs.
4. Discovery of Druggable Cryptic Pockets: The identified intermediate structures expose cryptic, druggable pockets at the interface between RBDs that are absent in both the stable Down and Up conformations. Virtual screening of FDA-approved drugs identified several candidates, including the anticancer drug nilotinib, that could potentially bind to these pockets, stabilize the intermediate state, and prevent the S-protein from adopting its active form.
5. Implications for Antibody Evasion and Neutralization: The accessibility of the receptor binding motif (RBM) and antibody epitopes varies significantly across different S-protein conformations. The newly identified 1UO state maximally exposes the RBM for ACE2 receptor binding while potentially shielding it from certain classes of antibodies, suggesting a mechanism for immune evasion. These findings support a "conformer selection" model for antibody binding, where antibodies recognize pre-existing flexible states of the S-protein.
